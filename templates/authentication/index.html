Under The Hood

The 5-Layer Pipeline

1

Gemini 2.5 Flash OCR

Google Gemini reads the prescription image with extended thinking (thinking_budget=4096). It transcribes exactly what is written — never correcting drug names. Handles Hindi/Devanagari script mixed with English, MAN notation (1-0-1 = morning + night), and circled numbers for duration.

2

LLM Drug Name Resolver (Groq · LLaMA 3.3 70B)

Garbled OCR names like "Sinarset" or "Montare LC" are sent to LLaMA 3.3 70B via Groq with the patient's age, weight, and all co-drugs as context. The model suggests the most likely real Indian brand name and generic (INN) name, with a confidence rating of high / medium / low.

3

RapidFuzz Local Matcher (Indian Medicine JSON)

The resolved name is fuzzy-matched against a local JSON database of Indian medicines using RapidFuzz's partial_ratio scorer. A score of 75+ marks the drug as Verified. This dataset is a current limitation — it is a static snapshot and does not include every regional brand or recent launch.

4

Clinical Plausibility Validator (Groq · LLaMA 3.3 70B)

A second LLM call checks whether each matched drug makes clinical sense for the patient's age, complaint, and other medicines. Implausible matches are demoted to Unverified and sent back for a second fuzzy-match attempt using the LLM's suggested correction.

5

Anakin Wire Price Fetcher (Parallel · On Demand)

When you click "Compare Prices", three Anakin Wire async jobs submit simultaneously — tm_search (Truemeds), nm_search (Netmeds), tmg_search (Tata 1mg). Results come back in ~2–3 seconds and are displayed as a sorted price catalog, cheapest first.

Questions

How accurate is the drug identification?

Honestly — it varies. For common Indian brand names (Dolo, Sinarest, Montair, Pantop, Pan-D) the system works well. For rare drugs, very regional brands, or badly smeared handwriting the accuracy drops significantly. This is an early MVP built on a static local medicine dataset of limited size. The confidence score on every card is there specifically so you can judge when to trust it and when to double-check with your pharmacist. We show everything — verified and unverified — so nothing is hidden from you.

What is RapidFuzz and why use it?

RapidFuzz is a fast fuzzy string matching library. We use it to compare the LLM-resolved drug name against our local Indian medicine database using a partial_ratio scorer. It handles misspellings, abbreviations, and partial matches well. The downside is that it is purely statistical — it doesn't understand medicine. A score of 76 might be a correct match or a wrong one. That's why we layer a clinical plausibility check on top. RapidFuzz handles the first-pass shortlist; the LLM validates whether it actually makes sense.

Why are some medicines shown as "Unverified"?

A drug is marked Unverified when either the fuzzy match score is below 75 out of 100, or when the clinical validator determines the match is implausible for the patient's age and condition. This happens most often with very regional brand names not in our database, extremely garbled handwriting that even Gemini cannot read clearly, or drugs prescribed by very unusual combinations. We show Unverified drugs anyway — hidden from the price section — because telling you something is uncertain is more useful than hiding it.

How does the price comparison work?

When you click "Compare Prices" on a verified medicine card, we simultaneously query Truemeds (tm_search), Netmeds (nm_search), and Tata 1mg (tmg_search) through the Anakin Wire API. All three run in parallel so the result comes back in roughly 2–3 seconds. Results are sorted cheapest first and the lowest price is tagged as "Best". The cheapest result is saved to your record so re-opening the panel is instant. Note: prices are scraped and may occasionally be stale — always verify on the pharmacy site before ordering.

Does this replace professional medical advice?

No — and we want to be direct about this. ClarityRx is a health literacy tool, not a diagnostic or clinical system. The AI summary explains what your prescription likely means in plain language. It does not account for your full medical history, allergies, or doctor-specific reasoning. The descriptions and summaries are generated by a general-purpose LLM, not reviewed by a pharmacist. Always follow your doctor's original instructions and confirm with a pharmacist if anything looks unexpected.

What languages does the prescription need to be in?

The OCR model (Gemini 2.5 Flash) handles English, Hindi/Devanagari, and mixed-script prescriptions. Drug names written in Roman characters (even garbled ones) are handled best. Pure Hindi-script prescriptions are phonetically romanised before processing. Regional scripts beyond Hindi are not reliably supported in the current version — this is a known limitation we aim to address in future updates.
